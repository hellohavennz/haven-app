# 5.3 – The British Constitution (Verified Reference)

## 🇬🇧 Verified Key Facts

- The **British Constitution** is not contained in a single written document but is formed from a combination of laws, conventions, and historical principles.  
- It is described as **“unwritten”** or more accurately **“uncodified”**, meaning that it is drawn from many sources rather than a single constitutional text.  
- The Constitution defines the **relationship between the state, its citizens, and the institutions of government**.  
- It establishes the powers of the **executive (government)**, **legislature (Parliament)**, and **judiciary (courts)**, ensuring that no branch has absolute authority.  
- The core principle of the Constitution is the **rule of law**, meaning that everyone — including government officials — is subject to the law.  
- The **monarchy**, while central to tradition, has limited powers and acts on the advice of ministers within the framework of constitutional convention.  
- The main sources of the British Constitution are:  
  1. **Statute law** — laws passed by Parliament (e.g., the **Human Rights Act 1998**, **Scotland Act 1998**, **Equality Act 2010**).  
  2. **Common law** — legal principles developed through court judgments.  
  3. **Constitutional conventions** — unwritten practices that guide political behaviour, such as the Prime Minister being a member of the House of Commons.  
  4. **Works of authority** — writings by constitutional scholars (e.g., **A.V. Dicey** and **Erskine May**) that explain and interpret the Constitution.  
  5. **Royal prerogatives** — historic powers of the Crown now exercised by ministers, such as issuing passports or declaring war.  
- The **separation of powers** ensures checks and balances between branches of government.  
- The **sovereignty of Parliament** is a key feature, meaning Parliament is the ultimate legal authority in the UK.  
- The **Bill of Rights (1689)** and **Act of Settlement (1701)** established principles of constitutional monarchy and parliamentary supremacy.  
- The Constitution also reflects the **devolution** of powers to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland, granting them authority over certain domestic matters.  
- The **Human Rights Act 1998** incorporated the **European Convention on Human Rights** into UK law, allowing citizens to challenge government actions in domestic courts.  
- The **Supreme Court of the United Kingdom**, established in **2009**, upholds constitutional principles and ensures the legality of government decisions.  
- The **European Union (Withdrawal) Act 2018** redefined the relationship between UK and EU law after Brexit, reaffirming parliamentary sovereignty.  
- The Constitution is **flexible**, adapting to social, political, and legal change without the need for a single formal amendment process.  
- The UK’s uncodified system relies heavily on trust, precedent, and political accountability to maintain democratic stability.  
- The Constitution embodies continuity and evolution — maintaining ancient traditions while adapting to modern governance.

## 💡 Concept Summary (for Memory Hook)

The British Constitution can be imagined as a **living framework** — a structure built over centuries, flexible enough to change but strong enough to uphold democracy and the rule of law. Its strength lies in balance, trust, and adaptation rather than rigid form.
