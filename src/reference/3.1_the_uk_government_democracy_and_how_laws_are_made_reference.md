# 3.1 – The UK Government, Democracy, and How Laws Are Made (Verified Reference)

## 🇬🇧 Verified Key Facts

- The United Kingdom is a **constitutional monarchy** and a **parliamentary democracy**.  
- The **monarch** is the **Head of State**, while the **Prime Minister** is the **Head of Government**.  
- The **UK Parliament**, based at **Westminster in London**, is the supreme law-making body of the country.  
- Parliament consists of **two Houses**:  
  - The **House of Commons**, made up of Members of Parliament (MPs) elected by the public.  
  - The **House of Lords**, composed of life peers, bishops, and a small number of hereditary peers.  
- The **monarch** formally approves all new laws through the process known as **Royal Assent**.  
- The **Prime Minister** leads the government, chairs the **Cabinet**, and is responsible for major policy decisions.  
- The **Cabinet** is made up of senior ministers who head government departments such as defence, health, and education.  
- Government ministers are supported by the **Civil Service**, which implements policies and manages public administration.  
- Members of the opposition form a **Shadow Cabinet**, which scrutinises government actions and offers alternative policies.  
- The UK holds **general elections** at least every five years to elect MPs to the House of Commons.  
- The country is divided into **constituencies**, each represented by one MP.  
- The UK uses the **“first-past-the-post”** electoral system, in which the candidate with the most votes in each constituency wins.  
- Citizens aged **18 or over** who are registered to vote may participate in general and local elections.  
- **Political parties** play an essential role in organising campaigns, forming governments, and representing public interests.  
- The main UK-wide political parties include the **Conservative Party**, **Labour Party**, and **Liberal Democrats**, alongside other regional parties such as the **Scottish National Party (SNP)** and **Plaid Cymru**.  
- **New laws**, or **Bills**, can be proposed by the government or by individual MPs and Lords.  
- A Bill must be approved by both Houses and then receive Royal Assent before it becomes an **Act of Parliament**.  
- The **judiciary** operates independently of Parliament and government, ensuring the **rule of law** and protecting citizens’ rights.  
- **Public accountability** is maintained through mechanisms such as parliamentary questions, select committees, and media scrutiny.  
- Citizens can influence government decisions by voting, joining political parties, petitioning Parliament, or engaging in lawful protest.

---

## 💡 Concept Summary (for Memory Hook)

The UK system of democracy can be imagined as a **circle of accountability** — Parliament creates the laws, the government enforces them, and the courts uphold them. Voters sit at the centre of this circle, choosing who governs and ensuring that power remains balanced and responsible.
