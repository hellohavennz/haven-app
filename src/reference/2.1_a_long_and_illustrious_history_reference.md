# 2.1 – A Long and Illustrious History (Part 2) (Verified Reference)

## 🇬🇧 Verified Key Facts

- The **18th century** marked the expansion of trade, empire, and science in Britain, setting the foundation for global influence.  
- The **Industrial Revolution** began in Britain around **1760**, transforming manufacturing, transport, and communication.  
- Key innovations included the **steam engine**, **spinning jenny**, and improvements in iron and coal production, which enabled rapid urbanisation and economic growth.  
- Cities such as **Birmingham**, **Manchester**, and **Glasgow** became major industrial centres.  
- The **slave trade**, which had been part of British commerce, was abolished by the **Abolition of the Slave Trade Act 1807**, and slavery itself was made illegal in the British Empire by the **Slavery Abolition Act 1833**.  
- The **19th century** (Victorian era) was a time of global expansion, social reform, and technological progress.  
- **Queen Victoria**, who reigned from **1837 to 1901**, oversaw the growth of the **British Empire**, which became the largest empire in history.  
- Britain’s economy and navy made it the world’s foremost industrial and colonial power.  
- Key reforms during the 19th century included the **Reform Acts (1832, 1867, 1884)**, which extended the right to vote to a wider portion of men, though women were still excluded.  
- The **Education Act 1870** introduced compulsory elementary education, improving literacy across the country.  
- The **Factory Acts** and other social legislation improved working conditions for children and industrial workers.  
- The **Irish Question** — political and social tensions between Ireland and Britain — remained significant throughout the 19th century, leading to calls for Irish Home Rule.  
- The **20th century** began with Britain as a global power, but its position was challenged by the **First World War (1914–1918)**.  
- The war led to the loss of many lives, widespread destruction, and major changes in British society.  
- The **Representation of the People Act 1918** granted the vote to most men over 21 and to women over 30 who met property qualifications.  
- The **Representation of the People Act 1928** later gave equal voting rights to women and men aged 21 and over.  
- After the First World War, Britain faced economic difficulties, unemployment, and social unrest.  
- The **Second World War (1939–1945)** had a profound impact on the UK; **Winston Churchill** became Prime Minister in 1940 and inspired the country during its defence against Nazi Germany.  
- The British people endured the **Blitz**, when major cities were bombed, but the war ended with victory for the Allied powers in 1945.  
- Post-war Britain saw reconstruction and the establishment of the **welfare state**, including the **National Health Service (NHS)** in **1948**.  
- The **British Empire** evolved into the **Commonwealth of Nations**, a voluntary association of independent countries.  
- The **1950s–1960s** saw decolonisation, with many countries gaining independence peacefully.  
- The **European Economic Community (EEC)**, later the **European Union (EU)**, influenced trade and policy, though the UK eventually voted to leave the EU in **2016**.  
- Britain remains a constitutional monarchy, a democracy, and a nation shaped by centuries of political, social, and technological change.

## 💡 Concept Summary (for Memory Hook)

Modern Britain’s story can be viewed as a **chain of transformation** — from industrial power to democratic reform, from empire to partnership. Each historical link represents adaptation: industrial innovation, global leadership, and a commitment to fairness and freedom that continue to define the UK’s national identity today.
