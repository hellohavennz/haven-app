# 5.4 – Fundamental Principles (Verified Reference)

## 🇬🇧 Verified Key Facts

- The United Kingdom’s democracy is founded on a set of **fundamental principles** that define how power is exercised and how citizens relate to the state.  
- These principles ensure that government remains accountable, fair, and representative of the people.  

### 1. Parliamentary Sovereignty  
- **Parliament** is the supreme legal authority in the UK.  
- It can make or repeal any law, and no Parliament can bind a future one.  
- Courts cannot overrule Acts of Parliament, although they can interpret them.  
- This principle gives elected representatives the ultimate authority to shape national policy.

### 2. The Rule of Law  
- Everyone, including public officials, is subject to the law.  
- Laws must be applied fairly and consistently, without arbitrary power or discrimination.  
- The judiciary ensures that laws and government actions conform to legal standards.  
- The principle was established in key historical documents such as the **Magna Carta (1215)** and reinforced by modern human rights legislation.

### 3. Democracy and Representation  
- The UK is a **parliamentary democracy**, meaning government derives its authority from the consent of the governed.  
- Citizens elect representatives to Parliament and local councils to make decisions on their behalf.  
- **Free and fair elections** ensure that power can change hands peacefully.  
- The **secret ballot**, introduced by the **Ballot Act 1872**, protects voters from coercion.  
- Political parties compete in elections, offering voters distinct policy choices.

### 4. Government Accountability  
- Ministers are accountable to Parliament and, through it, to the public.  
- The **Prime Minister’s Questions (PMQs)** session allows MPs to scrutinise government actions directly.  
- **Select committees** investigate specific areas of policy and administration.  
- Transparency and media freedom are essential for holding power to account.

### 5. Equality and Individual Rights  
- The **Equality Act 2010** protects individuals from discrimination based on characteristics such as age, race, gender, religion, or disability.  
- The **Human Rights Act 1998** guarantees fundamental freedoms such as the right to life, free expression, and a fair trial.  
- The UK’s approach balances individual rights with collective responsibility and public order.  

### 6. The Constitutional Monarchy  
- The **Monarch** remains Head of State but acts according to constitutional convention and ministerial advice.  
- This arrangement ensures continuity and national unity while leaving day-to-day governance to elected officials.  

### 7. Devolution and Local Governance  
- Power is distributed between the **UK Parliament** and the **devolved governments** of Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.  
- Local authorities deliver essential services and give citizens a voice in community decisions.  

### 8. Political and Civic Participation  
- Citizens contribute to democracy by voting, joining political parties, volunteering, and engaging in public debate.  
- Civic participation strengthens democracy and reinforces shared values of fairness, respect, and mutual responsibility.  

---

## 💡 Concept Summary (for Memory Hook)

The UK’s fundamental principles can be viewed as the **pillars of democracy** — sovereignty, law, accountability, and rights. Together, they hold up the nation’s political structure, ensuring that government remains just, balanced, and answerable to the people.
