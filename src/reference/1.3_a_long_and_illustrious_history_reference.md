# 1.3 – A Long and Illustrious History (Verified Reference)

## 🇬🇧 Verified Key Facts

- The history of the United Kingdom stretches over thousands of years, shaping the nation’s laws, institutions, and identity.
- Britain’s earliest inhabitants were hunter-gatherers, followed by settlers who introduced farming around 6,000 years ago.
- The **Romans** invaded Britain in **AD 43** under **Emperor Claudius**, establishing towns, roads, and walls — including **Hadrian’s Wall** — to defend the frontier.
- After the Romans left in the 5th century, Britain saw invasions by **Angles**, **Saxons**, and **Jutes**, who established small kingdoms that eventually formed **England**.
- **Christianity** spread through Britain during this period, influencing culture, education, and governance.
- In **AD 789–1066**, **Vikings** from Scandinavia raided and settled in parts of Britain, leaving a lasting impact on language and place names.
- The **Norman Conquest** in **1066**, led by **William of Normandy (William the Conqueror)**, transformed the English monarchy, law, and land ownership.
- Norman rule introduced **feudalism**, stronger central government, and many French words into the English language.
- In **1215**, **King John** signed the **Magna Carta**, which limited royal power and established that the monarch must obey the law — a cornerstone of British democracy.
- The **Hundred Years’ War** (1337–1453) with France and the **Wars of the Roses** (1455–1485) between rival royal houses shaped the monarchy’s power.
- The **Tudor period** (1485–1603) saw major change: **Henry VIII** established the **Church of England** in the 1530s, separating it from the authority of the Pope.
- The **Elizabethan era** (1558–1603) was a time of exploration, cultural growth, and the beginning of English influence overseas.
- The **Stuart period** introduced tensions between monarchy and Parliament, leading to the **English Civil War (1642–1651)** and the temporary abolition of the monarchy.
- The **Bill of Rights (1689)** confirmed parliamentary supremacy and the principles of constitutional monarchy.
- The **1707 Act of Union** united England and Scotland into the **Kingdom of Great Britain**; in **1801**, Ireland joined to form the **United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland**.
- During the **18th and 19th centuries**, the UK became the world’s leading industrial and colonial power.
- The **Victorian era** (1837–1901) was marked by industrial progress, empire expansion, social reform, and the strengthening of parliamentary democracy.
- British history includes movements for liberty, workers’ rights, and votes for women, shaping the modern democracy that exists today.

## 💡 Concept Summary (for Memory Hook)

The history of the UK can be imagined as a **tapestry woven over time**, with each era adding new threads — Roman order, Norman law, Tudor reform, and industrial progress — creating the national identity that exists today. Each thread represents continuity, adaptation, and the balance between tradition and change.
