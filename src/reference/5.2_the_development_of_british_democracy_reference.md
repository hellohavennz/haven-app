# 5.2 – The Development of British Democracy (Verified Reference)

## 🇬🇧 Verified Key Facts

- British democracy developed gradually over many centuries, shaped by key events, documents, and institutions that established the rule of law and representative government.  
- The foundations of democracy in the UK can be traced back to the **Magna Carta (1215)**, signed by **King John**.  
  - It established that the monarch was not above the law.  
  - It granted certain rights to subjects and limited royal power.  
- The **Model Parliament** of **1295**, under **King Edward I**, included representatives from counties and towns — an early form of elected assembly.  
- The **English Civil War (1642–1651)** arose from conflict between the monarchy and Parliament over the limits of royal authority.  
- The execution of **King Charles I** in **1649** led to the temporary establishment of a **Commonwealth** under **Oliver Cromwell**.  
- The **Restoration** of the monarchy occurred in **1660** under **King Charles II**, re-establishing royal power under parliamentary constraints.  
- The **Glorious Revolution (1688)** replaced **King James II** with **William III and Mary II**, confirming Parliament’s supremacy over the Crown.  
- The **Bill of Rights (1689)** established key democratic principles, including:  
  - The requirement that Parliament meet regularly.  
  - The right to free elections.  
  - The prohibition of cruel or unusual punishment.  
- The **Act of Union (1707)** joined England and Scotland to form **Great Britain**, with a single Parliament at Westminster.  
- The **Reform Acts** of the 19th and early 20th centuries progressively extended the right to vote:  
  - The **Reform Act 1832** granted voting rights to more men in the middle class and reformed unfair electoral districts.  
  - The **Reform Acts of 1867 and 1884** further widened suffrage to include many working men.  
  - The **Representation of the People Act 1918** granted voting rights to men over 21 and women over 30 who met property requirements.  
  - The **Equal Franchise Act 1928** gave men and women equal voting rights at age 21.  
  - The **Representation of the People Act 1969** reduced the voting age to **18** for both men and women.  
- The growth of **political parties**, such as the **Conservative Party**, **Liberal Party**, and later the **Labour Party**, gave voters clear choices and strengthened democratic competition.  
- The **Trade Union movement** played a major role in representing workers’ rights and influencing the rise of the Labour Party.  
- **Universal suffrage**, achieved in the 20th century, established the principle that all adults have an equal right to vote.  
- The **House of Lords Act 1999** reduced the number of hereditary peers, further democratising the legislative process.  
- Today, democracy in the UK is maintained through **free elections**, **independent courts**, and **freedom of expression**.  
- British democracy continues to evolve through public debate, legal reform, and participation in civic life.

## 💡 Concept Summary (for Memory Hook)

The growth of British democracy can be imagined as a **tree of liberty** — its roots in Magna Carta, its trunk formed by Parliament and law, and its branches representing the rights and freedoms earned over centuries of reform and public struggle.
